Crew Battle Format (Official Rules)

These rules summarize the official Esports Ohio SSBU crew battle format so you always know what’s happening and what’s expected in a match. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Match Structure

  • Format: 4v4 Crew Battle.
  • Each player has 3 stocks.
  • Each team has 12 total stocks per round.
  • All matches are Best of 3 rounds.

Game / Round / Series

  • Game: 1v1, 3-stock vs 3-stock.
  • Round: Sequence of games until all 12 stocks on one team are gone.
  • Series: Best of 3 rounds; win 2 rounds to win the match.

Rosters

  • Rosters must have exactly 4 players to compete.
  • All players should use a wired (ethernet) connection.

Game Lobby Settings (Host / Home Team)

The home team creates the arena and shares the ID + password with the visiting team. Visiting players should double-check the rules before starting. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

  • Format: 1-on-1
  • Style: Stock
  • Stock: 3
  • Time Limit: 8 minutes
  • FS Meter: Off
  • Items: Off, None
  • Stage Hazards: Off
  • Launch Rate: 1.0x
  • Score Display: Off
  • Show Damage: Yes
  • Spirits: Off
  • Stage: Choice
  • Max Players: 2
  • Voice Chat: Off

Stage List

Starter Stages:

  • Battlefield
  • Final Destination
  • Pokémon Stadium 2
  • Smashville
  • Town & City

Counterpick Stages:

  • Kalos Pokémon League
  • Lylat Cruise
  • Small Battlefield
  • Yoshi’s Story
  • Hollow Bastion

Stage Selection Procedure

Game 1 (Starters only): 1–2–1 Striking

  1. Player A strikes 1 stage.
  2. Player B strikes 2 stages.
  3. Player A strikes 1 more stage.
  4. The remaining stage is played.

Games 2+ (Starters + Counterpicks):

  • Winner of the previous game bans 3 stages from the full list.
  • Loser selects from the remaining stages.
  • Winner must stay the same character.

Player Order (“First Up”)

  • Away Team declares their first player first.
  • Home Team then declares their first player.
  • In later rounds, the team that won the previous round declares first.
  • The “first up” player must be different each round.

Stock Carryover & SD Procedure

  • Winner keeps the number of stocks they had remaining.
  • At the start of the next game, winner must SD until their stocks match their previous remaining stock count.
  • During SD: no attacks, no taunts, no charging moves or movement to gain advantage.
  • Play resumes only after invincibility ends and both players reset to neutral positions.
  • If a player SDs too many times, play continues from that stock state.

Timeouts & Sudden Death

  • If time runs out, player with more stocks wins.
  • If stocks are tied, player with lower % wins.
  • If both stocks and % are tied, play a 1-stock, 3-minute tiebreaker on the same stage with the same characters.

Character & Tech Restrictions

  • Amiibos are prohibited.
  • DLC fighters must be released for at least 1 week before use.
  • Mii Fighters must be Guest Miis and declared by type + specials (e.g., “Gunner 1321”).
  • Alternate stage forms are not allowed.
  • Disconnections and controller issues are mostly the player’s responsibility; repeated issues can cause forfeits.

Crew Battle Strategy Guide

Knowing the rules is step one. Step two is using them to win. This section explains how to build a smart lineup, when to use certain players, and how to think about stocks and stages as a team.

Opener

Usually a solid, consistent player who:

  • Plays well with minimal info on the opponent.
  • Doesn’t tilt easily if things go bad early.
  • Has strong general neutral & safe game plan.

Counterpick Player

These players shine when they know the matchup:

  • Can pick stages that fit their character perfectly.
  • Play characters that hard-counter specific archetypes.
  • Are comfortable coming in down or up stocks.

Anchor

Your last player, often your most clutch:

  • Can stay calm when the team is behind.
  • Has strong kill confirms and ledge trapping.
  • Comfortable in high-pressure, 1-stock situations.

Building a Lineup

  1. Decide your main win conditions: “We win by consistent neutral,” “We win by strong edgeguards,” “We win by one or two dominant matchups.”
  2. Place your most stable player as opener in most matches.
  3. Keep at least one hard counterpick in the middle of the lineup for specific characters.
  4. Use your anchor for either big comebacks or to close out tight rounds.
  5. Do not rely on one player to do everything. Crew battles reward teams, not just superstars.

Stocks = Team Resource

Think of stocks as a shared pool, not just individual lives.

  • Trading 2 stocks for 3 is good, even if you “lose” that game.
  • If you are up a full stock, you don’t need to force risky edgeguards.
  • If you are behind multiple stocks, it can be worth taking a calculated risk for a big edgeguard or read.

Between games, talk as a team: “Do I play safe and save stocks, or push for a big swing?”

Stage & Counterpick Logic

  • Pick stages that highlight your strengths (combos, kill power, zoning).
  • Avoid stages that remove your win condition (e.g., no platforms for ladder combos).
  • When behind, consider stages that create chaos your character benefits from.

Use the Stage Planning section below to write out preferred stages for each of your mains and secondaries.

Tournament Mindset & Game Plan

Being “good at friendlies” doesn’t automatically translate to bracket success. You need a simple routine that you follow every time you play a set.

Before the Set

  • • Take a deep breath and slow your thoughts.
  • • Decide your starting character.
  • • Know your preferred starter stage & what you want to ban.
  • • Pick 1–2 goals (e.g., “ledge trap more patiently”).

During the Set

  • • Watch their habits in neutral, ledge, and disadvantage.
  • • Don’t autopilot—adjust to what they’re actually doing.
  • • Use safe pressure instead of constant all-ins.
  • • Stay aware of kill ranges for both characters.

After the Set

  • • Write down 1 thing you did well.
  • • Write down 1 habit to fix.
  • • If you lost, give yourself a short cooldown before friendlies.
  • • Plan what to practice before your next event.

Stage & Counterpick Planning

Stage choice is a big part of competitive Smash. Knowing where your character is strong and where your opponent is uncomfortable can swing a whole round.

Your Stage Notes

For your main, fill this out (mentally or on paper):

  • 2–3 favorite starter stages and why.
  • 1–2 strong counterpicks that fit your win condition.
  • 1–2 stages you usually want to ban.

Example: “Mario – Likes triplats for combo ladders; bans big flat stages vs zoners.”

Opponent Stage Habits

Common stage tendencies by archetype:

  • Swordies: want space & mid platforms (PS2, Town & City).
  • Zoners: like bigger open stages (Kalos, FD).
  • Combo characters: want platforms to extend strings (Battlefield, Yoshi’s Story).

You can use this even when you don’t fully know the matchup. Think about what they gain from each stage.

Stock Trading & Risk Management

In crew battles, stocks matter more than “who won that game.” Sometimes you win even when your character explodes offstage—if you took enough stocks first.

When to Push

  • You are behind by a full stock or more and need a swing.
  • They are offstage with no jump and a weak recovery.
  • You’ve clearly seen a habit (always roll in, always jump from ledge).
  • Your character gets huge reward from a read (e.g., spike or early kill).

These are times where a higher-risk edgeguard or hard read can be reasonable.

When to Chill

  • You are up a full stock or more.
  • You’re at kill percent and they’re at low percent.
  • You’re cornered and they are pressuring safely.
  • You’re the anchor and the team needs you to last as long as possible.

Back off, reset to center stage, and force them to overextend first.

Pre-Match Checklist

Use this checklist before crew battle rounds or individual sets. The goal is to enter each match with a clear head and a clear plan.

  1. Deep breath: in for 4 seconds, hold for 2, out for 4.
  2. Confirm your character choice and backup plan.
  3. Know your preferred starter stage & what you’ll likely ban.
  4. Pick 1 neutral habit to focus on (e.g., “stop jumping from the corner”).
  5. Pick 1 advantage habit to focus on (e.g., “hold ledge trap, don’t rush in”).
  6. Remind yourself: the goal is to learn and improve, not just to panic about winning.
  7. Say out loud (or in your head): “I will stay calm, I will adapt, and I will use what I’ve practiced.”

Use this page before crew battles and tournaments. A consistent process leads to consistent results.
